# Pro brief — Hankel support-lattice termination (open-ended)

*Self-contained; no external references needed. You may pursue a full proof,
a counterexample, or a conditional reduction — whichever the evidence favors.
You are free to reformulate.*

## 1. Setup (elementary, finite fields)

Let `p` be a prime, `n | p-1`, and `mu_n = <g>` the group of `n`-th roots of
unity in `F_p` (`x_a = g^a`, `a in Z/n`). Reed–Solomon `RS_k` = polynomials
of degree `< k` evaluated on `mu_n`. Fix rate `1/2`: `k = n/2`. (Prize scale:
`n = 2^41`, `p < 2^256`; all questions below are asymptotic in `n`.)

**The Hankel syndrome system.** For a received pair `(u,v) in (F_p^n)^2` and a
slope `Z in F_p`, set `w = u + Z v` and let `S = (S_0,...,S_{n-k-1})` be its
length-`(n-k)` syndrome window (`S_m = sum_a w_a x_a^{m}` on the parity range;
the exact offset is immaterial below). For a split `t + j = n - k` form the
`t x (j+1)` **Hankel matrix**

```
    M(Z)[i, c] = S[i + c],   0 <= i < t,   0 <= c <= j.
```

Its right kernel `P := ker M(Z) subset F_p^{j+1}` is a space of **locator
polynomials** `ell(X) = sum_{d=0}^{j} ell_d X^d` of degree `<= j` (the classical
key equation / Berlekamp–Massey kernel). `P` is a linear subspace; call it a
**Hankel-kernel flat**. Every such `P` (over all `S in F_p^{n-k}`, all splits)
is in scope; the syndrome map is onto, so `S` ranges over all of `F_p^{n-k}`.

## 2. Sparse dual words and the support lattice

Evaluate `P`: to each point `x_a` attach the **column**
`col(a) = (ell(x_a))_{ell in basis(P)} in F_p^{dim P}`. A **sparse dual word of
weight `r`** is a size-`r` set `A = {a_1,...,a_r} subset Z/n` whose columns are
linearly dependent with all `r` involved and no proper subset dependent (a
minimal-support codeword of the dual of `P`'s evaluation code). `support(A) = A`.
Weight 2 = a pair of proportional columns.

Let `W(P)` be the family of minimal sparse-word supports of `P`, and

```
    L(P) := { empty } cup { unions of nonempty subfamilies of W(P) }
```

the **support lattice** (its elements are the union-closed sets).

## 3. The reduction (this step is already PROVED — take it as given)

A descent algorithm resolves the governing incidence conjecture ("Conjecture F")
by recursively peeling sparse dual words off a flat, each step dropping degree.
The following accounting identity is proven (memoized descent; states biject
with reachable closed sets):

> **Accounting identity.** The descent tree on a flat `P` has size at most
> `#L(P) * (1 + Wmax(P))`, where `Wmax(P) <= n` is the max words at any
> sub-instance; every root-to-leaf chain has length `<= dim P + (degree drop)`.

Hence **Conjecture F terminates in poly(n) for the Hankel family iff**

```
    MAX over Hankel-kernel flats P (rate 1/2, mu_n) of  #L(P)   is  poly(n).
```

This is trivial for MDS / generic-position flats (proven separately) and for
"fiber flats". The Hankel family is the one open case. **The entire question is
the growth of `max_P #L(P)`.**

## 4. What is known and observed

**(a) A discarded guess.** It was conjectured that every Hankel sparse-word
support is a union of cosets of a pullback `x -> x^M`, `M | gcd(n,k)` (which
would force `#L(P)` poly by a coset count). This is **FALSE**: at
`n=16, k=8, j=5, t=3` over `F_17` there is a weight-2 word on support
`{2,3}` (adjacent exponents `g^2, g^3`) — not a coset pair.

**(b) Numerical census (`F_17`, `n=16`, exhaustive-sample).**
- Weight-2 supports occur at **all** cyclic exponent-diameters roughly
  uniformly (matching the random-`dim`-`3`-flat baseline). So supports are
  **not** low-diameter; the local non-coset words of (a) are generic, not
  special.
- **But** a generic flat carries `<= 1` sparse word, hence `#L(P) <= 2`
  (trivial). The count `#{sparse words}` per flat is what varies.
- Therefore `max_P #L(P)` is controlled by **rare, highly structured flats**
  carrying many sparse words — not by the generic ones. (A larger `mu_32`,
  `mu_64` max-search is in progress; I can supply its output.)

**(c) Working conjecture (please confirm, refute, or replace).** The maximum
`#L(P)` is attained by **coset-structured** flats (invariant under some
`x -> x^M`), whose support lattice is the coset lattice — of size polynomial in
`n`; and **non-coset words are isolated** (each contributes `O(1)` to the
closure), so they never assemble a large lattice. If true, `max_P #L(P) =
poly(n)` and the Hankel family terminates.

**(d) The natural tool.** Hankel matrices have **displacement rank <= 2**:
`Z H - H Z^T` has rank `<= 2` (`Z` = down-shift). The classical consequence
(Padé / minimal partial realization / the Euclidean algorithm) is that a Hankel
kernel is generated by an essentially *single* locator pair — a strong rigidity
on how many independent sparse relations `P` can carry. This is the most likely
lever for bounding `#W(P)` and hence `#L(P)`.

## 5. The ask (choose your target)

Determine the growth of `max_P #L(P)` over rate-`1/2` Hankel-kernel flats on
`mu_n`. Any of the following is a complete, valuable deliverable:

- **(A) Full proof:** `max_P #L(P) <= poly(n)`. Most promising route: use the
  displacement-rank-`2` rigidity to bound the number of minimal sparse supports
  a Hankel kernel can carry (and/or to show the union-closure of its supports is
  poly-sized); handle the coset-structured extremal flats explicitly. This
  discharges the whole Conjecture-F chain.
- **(B) Counterexample:** a family of Hankel kernels (explicit `S`, split) with
  `#L(P)` super-polynomial in `n`. Equally valuable — it refutes the descent
  approach for the Hankel family and redirects the program. A construction with
  super-polynomially many minimal sparse supports, or a super-poly union-closure,
  suffices.
- **(C) Conditional:** `#L(P) <= poly(n)` modulo a clean, clearly-stated
  hypothesis (e.g. "a rate-`1/2` Hankel kernel has `<= C log n` minimal sparse
  supports", or a displacement-rank bound on dependent column families) —
  reducing termination to that single hypothesis.

Reformulation is welcome: if the right invariant is not `#L(P)` but, say, the
number of minimal supports, or a diameter-weighted count, say so and prove the
version that the accounting identity actually needs (`#closed-sets` poly, or
`#closed-sets * Wmax` poly).

## 6. Concrete anchors for sanity-checks

- `n=16, k=8, p=17, g=3`, split `t=3, j=5` (`t+j=8=n-k`). `P = ker M` is a
  `>= 3`-dim space of degree-`<=5` polynomials. The `{2,3}` weight-2 word of
  (a) lives here for a suitable `S`.
- Pullbacks available at `n=16, k=8`: `M in {2,4,8}` (`M | gcd(16,8)=8`); coset
  pairs are `{a, b}` with `a ≡ b (mod n/M)`.
