# Proof

Fix a rich target with `P(t)>=19`. Swapping the two product coordinates gives
at least `ceil(P(t)/2)>=10` distinct unordered representations. As in the
proved rich-fiber norm-cutoff theorem, at most two are diagonal and at most
one contains the root `-1`. Hence at least seven corresponding half-basis
vectors, say `v_1,...,v_7`, satisfy

```text
||v_i||_2^2<=3.                                      (1)
```

These seven vectors are distinct. Indeed, equality of two vectors would give
equality of the corresponding characteristic-zero shifted products, and the
proved dyadic shifted-product Sidon theorem would make the unordered root
pairs equal.

Every squared distance between two vectors is even. The proved distance-two
2-primary exclusion applies because all seven representations have one common
product modulo the odd row prime. Hence each distance is at least four. Let
`e` be the number of the 21 pairs whose squared distance is at most six. Every
other distance is at least eight. The centroid identity and `(1)` give

```text
4e+8(21-e)
  <= sum_(i<j)||v_i-v_j||_2^2
   = 7 sum_i ||v_i||_2^2-||sum_i v_i||_2^2
  <= 7*21=147.
```

Thus `168-4e<=147`, so `e>=6`. A matching on seven vertices has at most three
edges. The graph of distance-at-most-six pairs therefore has a vertex of
degree at least two. Call its rooted star `(E;F,G)`. This proves `(ISR2)`.

All three shifted products reduce to the same target modulo the selected
degree-one prime ideal

```text
P=(p,zeta-g).
```

Each shifted product is divisible by `pi^2`, so both normalized differences
in `(ISR1)` are algebraic integers. The ideal-batching theorem gives

```text
J=(beta_F-beta_E,beta_G-beta_E) subset (pi)^2 P.
```

Dividing by `(pi)^2` yields `K_(E;F,G) subset P`. Therefore

```text
p=N(P) divides N(K_(E;F,G)).                         (2)
```

Each principal ideal generated by a normalized difference is contained in
`K_(E;F,G)`, so its principal norm is divisible by the ideal norm. The
Parseval and AM-GM argument from the norm-cutoff theorem bounds either
unnormalized principal norm by `6^(n/4)`. Since `N((pi)^2)=4`, division by
`pi^2` divides the absolute principal norm by four. Combining these facts
with `(2)` proves `(ISR4)` and hence `(ISR3)`.

For an odd residue `r mod n`, Galois dilation sends `pi` to
`1-zeta^r=pi u_r`, where `u_r` is a cyclotomic unit. Thus the rooted-star
ideal of the dilated triple is `u_r^2 sigma_r(K_(E;F,G))`.
Absolute ideal norm is unchanged, and the Parseval distance is unchanged by
the same conjugate permutation used in the single-norm router. Exchanging the
leaves only exchanges the two generators. The remaining listed exchanges do
not change a shifted product. This proves orbit completeness. QED.
